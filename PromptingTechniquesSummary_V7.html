A comprehensive guide to effectively communicate with Large Language Models.

Prompting Technique Use It For How to Use It Example
1. Zero-Shot Prompting Simple, straightforward tasks where the model's general knowledge is sufficient. Just state your request directly; no examples needed. Write a haiku about a rainy day.
2. One-Shot Prompting Teaching the model a specific format, style, or specific custom behavior with a single example. Provide one example of an input-output pair before your actual query. Make this sentence sound more formal: 'Hey, wanna grab coffee?' -> 'Would you care to join me for a cup of coffee?'
3. Few-Shot Prompting Guiding the model on complex patterns, nuanced instructions, or specific data transformations by providing several examples. Provide multiple (2-5 typically) input-output examples before your actual query. Here are some examples to classify sentiment:
- I love this! -> Positive
- It's okay. -> Neutral
- I hate it.-> Negative
4. Prompt Chaining Breaking down a large, complex task into smaller, sequential sub-tasks, where the output of one step becomes the input for the next. This is a workflow: send a prompt, get output, use that output in the next prompt. No specific phrase in a single prompt. Prompt 1: "Summarize the following article: [Article Text]" Prompt 2 (using output from Prompt 1): "Based on this summary: [Summary Text], draft a 3-point email to stakeholders."
5. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Solving complex problems that require logical, step-by-step reasoning (e.g., math word problems, multi-step logic). Add instructions like "Let's think step by step," "Walk me through your reasoning," or "Show your work." If John has 5 apples and gives 2 to Sarah, then buys 3 more, how many apples does John have? Let's think step by step.
6. Tree-of-Thought (ToT) Exploring multiple possible solutions, evaluating alternatives, and self-correcting or selecting optimal paths in complex problem-solving or decision-making. Instruct the model to generate multiple options for a given step, evaluate them, and then choose the best one. Optionally, ask for the output to be able to backtrack if a path proves unpromising. For the product launch, brainstorm 3 distinct market entry strategies. For each strategy, evaluate and analyze its pros and cons regarding ROI and potential risks. Then, choose the single most promising strategy and justify your choice.
7. Meta Prompting Using the LLM to help you refine, optimize, or generate better prompts based on your goals and desired output characteristics. Ask the LLM to act as a "prompt engineer" or "prompt optimizer." Provide your objective and initial prompt (or idea), and ask for improvements. Act as a prompt engineer. My goal is to get a short, engaging social media post. Here's my draft prompt: 'Write about our new product.' How can I improve this prompt using the T.C.R.E.I. framework to get a better result?
8. Role-Playing / Persona Prompting Tailoring the output's tone, style, knowledge, and perspective to a specific audience or scenario. Begin the prompt by defining a clear persona for the LLM (e.g., "Act as a marketing expert," "You are a friendly customer service agent"). Act as a motivational coach. Write a short paragraph encouraging someone who feels stuck in their career to take the next step.
9. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) - Prompting Simulation Grounding responses in specific, provided external information to ensure factual accuracy and avoid hallucinations. Provide relevant context (documents, data, snippets) within the prompt itself and instruct the model to use only this information for its response. Context: Product X was launched last year, sales: Q1=$1M, Q2=$1.5M, Q3=$1.2M, Q4=$1.8M. Task: Based only on the provided sales data, describe the sales trend for Product X last year.
10. Constraint-Based Prompting Ensuring the LLM's output adheres to specific rules, formats, lengths, or content inclusions/exclusions. Clearly state all the rules and limitations in your prompt using keywords like "must," "only," "avoid," "max X words," "in JSON format." Write a 100-word product description for a new eco-friendly water bottle. It must highlight sustainability. Do not mention plastic. Include a call to action to visit our website.
11. Least-to-Most Prompting Solving very complex problems by breaking them into a series of smaller, dependent sub-problems, with each step building on the last. Guide the model through each sub-problem sequentially, feeding the output of one step into the prompt for the next. Problem: Evaluate the profitability of selling 500 units of Product A given its costs and pricing. Step 1: Calculate total revenue for 500 units at $20/unit. Step 2: Calculate total variable costs for 500 units at $8/unit. Step 3: Calculate total fixed costs ($1,500). Step 4: Calculate net profit using results from steps 1, 2, and 3.
12. Self-Consistency Improving reliability and accuracy by generating multiple diverse reasoning paths and selecting the most consistent answer. Instruct the model to generate multiple attempts at solving a problem (often with CoT), then analyze and select the most common result. (Often requires multiple turns or an external aggregation logic). Solve the following riddle. Generate 3 different step-by-step reasoning paths that lead to an answer. Then, state the final answer that appears most frequently among your reasoning paths.
13. Self-Improving / Self-Refinement Critiquing and refining its own generated output based on specified criteria, leading to higher quality and more polished responses. Instruct the model to critically evaluate its previous response, identify flaws or missing elements, and then revise/regenerate based on that critique. Generate an initial proposal for [X]. Now, assume you are a quality assurance specialist. Review your proposal for logical consistency, completeness, and clarity. List any issues found. Finally, revise the original proposal to address these issues.
14. Maieutic (Socratic) Prompting Guiding the model to a deeper understanding, self-correction, or discovery of an answer through a series of probing questions. Ideal for complex, ambiguous, or open-ended problems. Ask follow-up questions that challenge, clarify, or expand on the model's previous responses. Encourage it to explain its reasoning, consider counter-arguments, or break down its solution. State three reasons we should launch in Japan first. Now challenge each reason with one counter-argument. Finish with your final verdict in 2 lines.
